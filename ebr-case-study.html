01 — The Problem

Proving value at scale is hard when you're doing it one meeting at a time

Traditional Executive Business Reviews at Zywave were exactly what the name implies: scheduled calls or in-person meetings where a Customer Success Manager would walk clients through their product usage, celebrate wins, and make a case for renewal. They worked — but they didn't scale.

With a book of business around 12,000 customers, the math didn't work. There weren't enough hours in the week to have meaningful, data-informed conversations with every account. Smaller customers either didn't get reviews at all, or got a shallow version. The CS team was spending significant time on meeting prep: pulling usage data manually, formatting it, and building slides that were different every time.

The question I kept coming back to: what if the email was the EBR?

The Core Insight
"Customers don't need a meeting to understand their value. They need the right data, framed the right way, at the right time."

02 — Research

Understanding what customers actually find valuable

The hardest design problem wasn't the email itself — it was figuring out what data to put in it. Zywave had plenty of usage data available through Heap, but availability and value aren't the same thing. I needed to understand which metrics actually resonated with agency owners versus which ones were just interesting to us internally.

I approached this from multiple angles: customer surveys and feedback to understand what language they used about their own business success, interviews with CS team members who ran EBRs regularly (they had pattern-matched on what questions customers asked most), and a competitive analysis to find analogues in the market. There weren't many direct competitors doing this in B2B SaaS — the closest reference was Spotify Wrapped, which reframes usage data as a personal story rather than a report.

Identified which metrics mapped to how customers described their own business goals — quoting speed, client retention, new business wins.
◈

The Central Design Challenge
Determining what data customers would find valuable versus what data was actually accessible — and finding the overlap.

03 — Version 1

Starting with two products and a clear frame

V1 launched in May 2023 with two products: TurboRater (quoting software) and Agency Matrix (agency management). The email structure was built around a simple but powerful frame: here's how the best agencies use this product, and here's where you stand.

Each email had two infographic sections. The first showed benchmark data — how many quotes our most successful TurboRater agencies completed per day, or how many attachments top Agency Matrix users added. The second showed the customer's own usage over the past calendar year. The juxtaposition was intentional: it made the benchmark feel aspirational rather than critical, and it made the customer's own data feel meaningful rather than abstract.

The copy took a careful tone — motivating without being condescending. The subject line, "How Do You Compare to Our Most Successful Partners?" was designed to trigger curiosity without feeling like a report card.

The QA process on V1 was meticulous. Before sending, I reviewed both email templates in detail — flagging spacing inconsistencies between infographic sections, copy errors ("Bridged" vs. "Bridges"), broken links in the footer, survey question phrasing, and sender address setup. The attention to craft in a high-volume automated email matters: even customers who won't consciously notice a well-edited email will notice a poorly edited one.

04 — Version 2

Expanding the model — and refining what worked

V2 launched in late 2024, adding two more products — Sales Cloud / miEdge and Zywave Content — and incorporating everything we'd learned from V1. The core structure held, but nearly every detail was refined.

The most significant content change was in how the benchmark was framed. V1 used daily activity metrics (quotes per day, attachments per day). V2 shifted to a 90-day power user threshold — a more durable and actionable benchmark that gave customers a clearer goal rather than a daily performance standard. The framing also softened: "By making a few thoughtful adjustments, you can increase your agency's advantage and achieve greater success" rather than V1's more transactional tone.

Operationally, V2 moved from the generic Gainsight sender to a branded Zywave customer success address, and added Microsoft Bookings as a direct CS scheduling option — closing the loop between the email and a human conversation for customers who wanted one.

05 — Performance

How the emails performed across 2025

Across all four products in 2025, the program sent 8,214 emails — part of a program designed to reach roughly 5,000 customers across Zywave's book of business. Estimated opens across the year totaled ~1,586, with open rates at or above the B2B industry average (20–25%) for V1 products. Click rates require a note on methodology.

The click rates recorded in Gainsight are calculated as percentage of openers, not percentage of total sends. The table below shows both figures — the platform-reported rate (clicks ÷ openers) and the true rate against total sends — so the numbers are honest and comparable.

The split between V1 and V2 products is meaningful even with the corrected numbers. V1 emails held strong open rates — the subject line and concept continued to work — but drove minimal downstream action (under 1% of total sends clicked through). V2 emails performed markedly better on both metrics: 2.0–2.5% click rate of total sends, and 12–14% of the openers who did engage went on to click. The 90-day power user benchmark and the Microsoft Bookings CTA gave customers something more concrete to do after reading.

06 — Process

How the work actually got done

1
Research: What do customers care about?
Customer surveys and feedback to understand their vocabulary and success metrics. CS team interviews to surface which data points drove the most engagement in live EBR conversations. The goal was to find the intersection of "data we have" and "data that matters to them."
2
Concept: The benchmark frame
The Spotify Wrapped analogy emerged during competitive analysis — not as a visual reference, but as a structural one. Reframing usage data as a story about where you stand relative to the best in the industry changed the emotional valence of the email from "report" to "opportunity."
3
Content design: Structure, copy, tone
Writing the email copy was a balance between motivating and honest. The benchmark had to feel aspirational, not shaming. The customer's own data had to feel meaningful, not clinical. Every word went through multiple rounds — including a detailed QA pass on both templates before launch, catching copy errors, broken links, spacing inconsistencies, and survey question phrasing.
4
Technical collaboration: Gainsight + Heap
Sean Leahy handled the Gainsight implementation — building the automation programs, configuring the merge tokens, and connecting Heap's usage data to the email template. My role was defining what the email needed to say and how the data needed to be structured; his was making it work at scale.
5
Iteration: V1 → V2
Performance data, customer feedback, and a year of live sends informed every V2 decision. The benchmark metric changed, the tone refined, the sender address professionalised, and the CTA deepened. Two new products were added. The program scaled from a pilot to a core CS touchpoint.

07 — Reflection

What this taught me about content design at scale

The EBR email started as a question about efficiency — how do you do more EBRs with less time? But it ended up being about something more interesting: what does it mean to communicate value proactively, before a customer has to ask for it?

The biggest design constraint wasn't writing or structure — it was data. Knowing what we could measure wasn't the same as knowing what was worth measuring. That decision, made early, shaped everything downstream. The benchmark frame only works if the benchmark is something customers actually recognize as meaningful to their business.

The V1→V2 shift confirmed something I suspected from the research: daily activity metrics feel like performance monitoring, but threshold-based power user benchmarks feel like a goal. The framing changed the relationship between the data and the reader. That's content design — not just what you say, but how the structure of what you say changes how people feel about it.

This project also reinforced how much the "translator" role matters on cross-functional work. The email required customer insight, content judgment, and technical implementation to all be in sync. My job was making sure that what customers needed, what the data could say, and what Gainsight could build all pointed in the same direction.
