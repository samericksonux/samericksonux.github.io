Building Zywave's first product writing style guide from scratch

Creating a unified voice across 12+ products to fix years of inconsistency caused by team turnover, company acquisitions, and missing standards.

Role
UX Writer
Timeline
~1–3 months (2018–2019)
Deliverable
Writing style guide within the Booster Design System
Audience
R&D team, designers, developers
The Problem

12+ products, zero writing standards

When I joined the UX team, Zywave had over a dozen products — many acquired from other companies — and no shared standards for how the UI communicated with users. Every product sounded different. Terminology varied wildly, especially between the commercial insurance and benefits insurance sides of the business, where the same concepts had entirely different names.

There was no process for who to ask about copy, no reference for how to write a button label or an error message, and no guidance for the developers and product managers who were often writing UI text themselves. The result was a fragmented, inconsistent experience that made Zywave's suite feel like a collection of unrelated tools rather than a cohesive platform.

Meanwhile, the marketing and content teams each had their own style guides — but nothing existed for the product experience itself.

The Approach

Research the landscape, then build for the real world

This was one of my first projects on the UX team, and I approached it as both a craft exercise and a research problem. I studied established writing style guides from Mailchimp, Google Material Design, Atlassian, and 18F, as well as foundational research from Nielsen Norman Group on UX writing best practices. I also reviewed Zywave's existing marketing and content style guides to find areas of alignment and to create cross-brand consistency where possible.

The guide needed to be practical above all else. The R&D team wouldn't adopt a 50-page document — they needed clear rules they could reference in the moment. So I focused on the decisions that came up most often: capitalization, punctuation, voice, terminology, and how to handle the inevitable tension between the style guide and what was already live in the product.

What the Guide Covers

Practical standards for real product decisions

◎

Voice & Tone

Defined Zywave's product voice as approachable, genuine, empathetic, clear, conversational, and knowledgeable — with guidance on how tone shifts by context while voice stays consistent.

◉

Grammar & Mechanics

Clear rules on sentence case, Oxford commas, when to skip periods, how to handle numbers, bolding vs. italics, active voice, and contractions — each tied to specific UI contexts.

▤

Terminology & Naming

Standardized terms across commercial and benefits insurance products — resolving conflicts where the same concept had different names across acquired products and business lines.

▢

Audience Awareness

Documented how the product audience differs from marketing's audience, and how copy needs to adapt across products with different user types (brokers, admins, clients).

◫

New vs. Old

A practical framework for handling the reality of design debt — guidance for when to match legacy product copy, when to update it, and how to find a middle ground.

◧

Error Messages

Created initial error message guidelines and patterns to bring consistency to how products communicated problems — later expanded by the team as capabilities grew.

Part of Something Bigger

Embedded in the Booster Design System

The writing style guide wasn't a standalone document — it shipped as the Voice and Tone section of Zywave's Booster Design System, sitting alongside components, patterns, visual standards, and developer documentation.

I was involved in the broader creation of Booster, contributing as a voice in the design process across the system. But the Voice and Tone section — the writing style guide, error message patterns, and messaging guidelines — was my domain.

Key Decisions & Impact

What made the guide stick

01

Practical over comprehensive

I prioritized the decisions people actually faced every day — capitalization, punctuation, terminology — over an exhaustive grammar reference. A guide that gets used beats a guide that's thorough but ignored.

02

Acknowledged the mess

The "New vs. Old" section was critical. Rather than pretending the guide would fix everything overnight, I gave practical guidance for navigating the gap between ideal standards and legacy reality.

03

Saved time across the team

Before the guide, developers and PMs came to me (or just guessed) for every copy question. After it shipped, the guide became the go-to reference — freeing my time for higher-impact work.

04

Created cross-product consistency

Standardizing terminology across commercial and benefits products made the Zywave suite feel like one platform instead of a patchwork of tools.

Reflection

Lessons from building writing standards in a growing org

Creating a style guide for a product suite built by different teams over many years is as much a diplomacy project as it is a writing project. You can't just declare standards — you have to make them useful enough that people want to follow them.

I'm proud that the guide is still live and in use years later as part of the Booster Design System. It was designed as a living document — "always growing and changing, just like language itself" — and that philosophy gave it longevity.

If I were to do it again, I'd invest more time in before-and-after examples for each rule. Showing "don't write this / write this instead" with real product screenshots would have made the guide even more immediately useful for developers working in specific product contexts.
