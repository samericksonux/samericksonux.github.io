The Problem

A support site nobody used — because nobody could

Zywave's existing support site was built on MadCap Flare, a technical publishing tool designed for documentation — not self-service experiences. Each product had its own isolated help site behind a login wall, meaning customers had to authenticate separately for each one. If a workflow crossed product boundaries, content was either duplicated across sites, linked to another gated site (requiring a second login), or simply not documented.

The layout was outdated and difficult to navigate. Users couldn't figure out where to go to find the information they needed, so they didn't bother trying. Instead, they defaulted to calling or emailing support directly. Many didn't even know the help site existed.

On the internal side, maintaining the site was equally painful. Updating just two numbers in a single article — a task that happened multiple times per year — took over two hours using MadCap Flare. Any edit, no matter how small, carried this same overhead, making it nearly impossible for the content team to keep articles current.

The Process

Research-led from discovery to launch

Phase 1 — Discovery & Stakeholder Alignment

Building the business case

I started by interviewing internal stakeholders across the support, product, and content teams. The publishing pain was immediately clear — I documented the real time cost of content updates (2+ hours per article edit) and the near-zero usage of the existing site. I presented these findings alongside the cost-neutral platform switch opportunity (Salesforce Experience Cloud was already included in Zywave's contract but unused) to the Chief Customer Officer and Chief Product Officer. Both agreed the move was a no-brainer.

Stakeholder InterviewsAnalytics ReviewCost Analysis
Phase 2 — User Research

Understanding why self-service was failing

I conducted interviews with Zywave customers, their end-user clients, and internal support agents who used the site daily. The quantitative data told us no one was using the old support site — and the interviews confirmed why. Customers didn't know it existed, and those who did found it too hard to navigate. They defaulted to contacting support directly rather than trying to self-serve.

User InterviewsSurveysSupport Ticket Analysis
Phase 3 — Competitive Analysis & IA

Learning from the best and restructuring from scratch

Since direct competitors' support sites were inaccessible, I analyzed support experiences that Zywave customers were most likely familiar with — Microsoft, Apple, and Salesforce. This research confirmed that a prominent search bar was essential, but also that users needed a clear browsing path for when they didn't know what to search for. A critical insight from studying Apple and Microsoft's support models was that their help articles were indexed by Google — meaning customers could find answers without ever visiting the support site directly. We made Google indexing a priority for the new platform. I then ran card sorting exercises that shaped the new site's information architecture.

Phase 4 — Build & Migration

Hundreds of articles, rebuilt by hand

With the new platform and structure defined, I led the effort to manually migrate hundreds of support articles from MadCap Flare to Salesforce Experience Cloud. Each article was reviewed, restructured to fit the new IA, and rewritten where needed. I coordinated with dev and UX team members to build out the platform in parallel.

Content MigrationPlatform BuildCross-team Coordination
Phase 5 — Testing & Rollout

Validating with users before going live

I ran usability testing sessions with customers to validate the new site's navigation, search behavior, and article findability before launch. The rollout was staged — first to internal teams, then to customers, and finally embedded as the live help resource within Zywave products.

Key Research Insights

What the research told us

01

The gate was the biggest barrier

Requiring login to access help content meant customers never discovered the resource existed. Removing the gate was the single highest-impact change.

02

Search is table stakes, but browsing is essential

Users who don't know the right search terms need a visual path through product categories to find their answer.

03

Internal users are users too

Support agents and content authors were as affected by the old site as customers. Reducing article update time from 2 hours to 2 minutes meant content could actually stay current.

04

Community needs critical mass to work

We built a community forum at launch, but it never gained traction — validating that community features need an active user base and dedicated moderation to succeed.

05

The business case writes itself when you show the pain

Demonstrating that it took 2+ hours to change two numbers in an article made executive buy-in immediate.

06

Product silos create content silos

The old per-product help sites meant cross-product workflows were either duplicated, fragmented, or not documented. A unified site finally let content reflect how customers actually work.

Reflection

What I'd do differently & what I'm proud of

This project taught me that research doesn't have to be a separate phase — it can be the engine that drives an entire initiative. As a UX Writer, I stepped well beyond my core role to lead research, build the business case, coordinate the migration, and shape the final experience.

The community forum was a lesson in knowing when to cut. The research supported the idea, but the execution context wasn't right.

What I'm most proud of is the lasting impact. I transitioned off the project in 2022, but the team continued building on the foundation — polishing the experience and shipping features from my backlog, like the search refinement filters I had scoped but couldn't implement before moving roles. The site's core architecture remains intact five years later.

Making help articles discoverable via Google was one of those decisions that seemed obvious in hindsight but required competitive research to surface. It meant customers could get answers without even knowing the support site existed — which is exactly what great self-service should feel like.
